Explaining Facebook's recent advertising technology updates

Atlas and LiveRail are Facebook companies that help advertisers and publishers show relevant ads on websites across the internet and in apps on your phone. Today we're updating their privacy policies to reflect recently announced new features of these services and to make the policies easier to understand.


One of the top things we hear from people i...s that they want ads they see to be more interesting – and at the same time, we want to improve ads to make them as relevant as possible. With these updates, we want to offer explanations of the changes and how the Atlas and LiveRail advertising technology works.


Atlas


Atlas launched in September 2014 to help advertisers reach potential customers with greater accuracy, while providing more relevant ads. Atlas also helps businesses measure whether their ads are effective.


We're updating Atlas' privacy policy to better explain how Atlas works and how information from third parties is used to improve the relevance of ads people see. 


Key updates:

  • Nothing in the updated Atlas privacy policy changes how the service works; instead the new policy provides more detail about some of the improvements we've previously announced. These include tools to measure the effectiveness of ads for driving online and offline purchases, as well to improve targeting, serving and measuring ad performance across devices.
  • The updated privacy policy provides more details of how we receive information from third parties to improve the ads people see. For example, an advertiser might want to reach people who have made a purchase on their website in the past with ads served by Atlas that contain a discount code for their next purchase.

LiveRail

As we announced at Facebook’s F8 conference last month, LiveRail will now include some information from Facebook to make sure the right ad is delivered to the right person at the right time. We're updating LiveRail's privacy policy to reflect this.


Key updates:

  • LiveRail's previous policy was focused on the experience for desktop computers. We've now outlined how we will use information from mobile devices to deliver more relevant ads. For example, LiveRail can limit the number of times a person sees the same ad on different apps that use LiveRail’s services, or use information about an ad someone interacts with in one app to show similarly interesting ads in another app.
  • To help publishers understand what ads are most relevant to their audiences and how their ads are working, we include language that explains how we look at ads served across devices, and how people interact with those ads (such as clicks, or visits to the advertiser’s website or app). While we help publishers measure how their ads perform, we do not tell advertisers or publishers who people are.

Putting People in Control


In addition to improving the relevance of ads, Atlas and LiveRail are also committed to giving people control over the ads they see. On Facebook, you can use ad preferences to learn why you’re seeing a particular ad and control what information we use to show you ads.

Currently, Atlas and LiveRail can use information from Facebook – like age and gender – to better serve and measure ads. Facebook de-identifies this information before it is used by Atlas or LiveRail. In the future, if Atlas and LiveRail use interest-based information provided by Facebook, Atlas and LiveRail are committed to honoring the ad preferences you share with Facebook.

For additional control, people can opt out of seeing ads from Atlas and LiveRail based on the apps and sites they use off Facebook through the Digital Advertising Alliance. People can also use the ad controls in their iOS or Android settings.   


Rob Sherman is Facebook's Deputy Chief Privacy Officer

Protecting your account made easy



Since last November, millions of people have visited Facebook Privacy Basics, a dedicated resource that gives people a simpler way to find and use our privacy controls. After seeing how interested people are in material like this, we discovered that they also had questions about our security tools.

...


Today we've expanded Privacy Basics to include 11 new visual and interactive guides. They focus on the tools we make available to help you secure your account, the steps we take to keep your information secure, and the ways you can recognize and avoid attempts to compromise your information. The content is available in 40 languages and ready to be accessed wherever you are—on your phone, desktop, or tablet.

This new security collection, “How to Keep Your Account Secure,” covers specific topics like how to create a better password, what to do if you see a suspicious post or message, what to do if your account has been taken over by someone else, how to recognize attempts to steal your passwords and info, how Facebook protects your information, and how we handle information requests from governments. Online threats are a growing concern for people, and our hope is that this helps people better understand how to stay safe and secure on Facebook.

Check out the new security content for Privacy Basics.

Paying good advice forward on Data Privacy Day

By Erin Egan
Chief Privacy Officer, Facebook

People love to share useful tips, whether it's letting someone else know about a discount, interesting story, or restaurant recommendation. As part of Data Privacy Day, we wanted to share some of the best privacy tips people pass on to their friends on Facebook.

We recently surveyed about 1,000 people who u...se Facebook and asked them how they find and share advice about privacy. Sixty one percent of people said they searched Facebook for privacy advice, but almost as many — 57 percent — asked their friends. Half of those who responded say they trust their close friends for this kind of information, a third look to their tech-savvy friends, and about 20 percent use mass media.

The privacy tips people share with friends, family, and others cover a broad range of topics. This underscores the fact that privacy doesn't have a universal definition — what's private to one person may not be private to another. By giving people control over how they share their information online, they can make the choices that are right for them. That concept is at the heart of how Facebook and other services design products and educational resources.

We've collected the tips that people say they share most frequently. If you find them useful, please take a moment as part of Data Privacy Day to share them:

Know your audience. Remember that you have control over who sees what. The most common advice people give is about who can see what. “Be careful what you share and who you share it with” is how one person put it. You can choose an audience every time you post on Facebook, change the audience of past posts, and update the audience for basic info on your profile.

Use security features. Making sure your information is secure is an important part of privacy. People recommended security tools like Login Alerts, where Facebook sends you a notification if someone logs into your account from a new place, as well as advice about changing passwords.

Get the facts. Be mindful of rumors. Many people say they help set the record straight with their friends, providing “clarification about false information and recommendations on improving privacy.” They advise their friends to fact-check memes and “read through” Facebook's privacy information “if you want to know what you are sharing.”

Take control. You have several options when someone or something bothers you on Facebook. People suggest tools for their friends about blocking people, hiding stories in News Feed, or removing tags in stories or photos. Facebook also offers social resolution tools, which help people address things they don't like seeing on Facebook, but that may not go against our Community Standards.

Check your settings. It's a good idea to periodically review your privacy and security settings, which people often suggest to their friends. We've also introduced tools like Privacy Checkup, which helps you review and control who you're sharing with.

Every day, people are paying good privacy advice forward on Facebook. We offer a wide range of tools and information to put you in control of your Facebook experience, and we will continue to build on this foundation in the year ahead. Please tell your friends — good advice about privacy on Facebook is worth paying forward.

September 4, 2014 ·
A Closer Look at a Checkup Helping Millions of People Control Who They Share With

Earlier this year, we announced a new Privacy Checkup that walks you through a few quick steps to help make sure only the people you want can see your stuff. Today, we're making it available around the world for people using Facebook on desktop.

When you use the Checkup, you can review who you're posting to on Facebook, which apps you're using, and ...the privacy of key pieces of information on your profile. Although people can find it any time from Privacy Shortcuts, we've begun suggesting it to people when they visit News Feed and we plan to suggest it to hundreds of millions of people on Facebook. 




We know people come to Facebook to connect with their friends, not with us. But we know how important it is for everyone to be in control of what they share and who they share it with. And of the people who have started the Checkup to so far, three in four have completed all three steps. This includes things like changing the audience for their posts or contact information, removing old apps, or updating information in their profile.

It's part of an ongoing effort to put more control in people's hands, at the moment they need it. Our first Privacy Checkup helped people posting publicly make sure they were sharing with the right audience. We've redesigned and simplified our audience selectors across Facebook on desktop and mobile—now they explain the audience right where people post.

There's more work for us to do here. We'll be listening closely to people's feedback about the new Checkup, improving it over time and thinking of new ways we can help everybody on Facebook share exactly what they want with who they want.

June 12, 2014 ·
Improving the Control You Have Over Advertising on Facebook

Today, we're announcing two updates to how ads work on Facebook for people in the U.S. First, we're improving our system to show you more relevant ads based on the apps and websites you visit off of Facebook. And second, we're launching a new ad preferences tool to help you understand why you are seeing a specific ad and to give you granular contro...ls to improve the ads you see. You can access ad preferences directly from every single ad on Facebook. By clicking on the upper right hand corner of an ad you can see why the ad is being shown to you and also choose which preferences influence the ads you see.


Here's some information on these changes and what they mean for you:


What information am I sharing with Facebook, and how is this different?

One of the things we've heard from people is that they want ads on Facebook to be more relevant. But how do we learn what you're actually interested in? In the past, we've shown you ads based on things you've done on Facebook like Pages you've liked and things you've clicked on. We've also shown messages from businesses that are trying to reach people who have previously visited their websites or have some other kind of relationship off Facebook.


Now, in order to show you the best ads possible, we're going to include some of the websites you visit and apps you use to better understand what you're interested in, like many companies already do on the web today. This happens automatically when you visit a website or app that has integrated Facebook functionality.


Let's say you're thinking about buying a new TV and starting your research on the web. In the past, we might show you an ad for a TV based on Pages you've liked and similar activity on Facebook. Or we might have shown you an ad for a TV from a specific marketer's site you visited. But now we can show you ads for TVs from other brands, which may offer a lower price. And because we think you’re interested in electronics, we may show you ads for other electronics in the future, like speakers or a game console to go with your new TV.


Do I have control over the kinds of information I'm sharing and the ads I see?

Yes. If you don't want us to use your activity on other websites and apps to show you better ads, you can use the Digital Advertising Alliance's opt-out tool, which you can find here: http://www.aboutads.info/choices/. This tool is supported by Facebook and by more than 100 other companies that show this kind of advertising online. We also honor limit ad tracking signals from your Android or iOS device if you have them enabled.


In addition, you can use our new ad preferences tool, which shows you the reason you're seeing any ad on Facebook and lets you control the interests associated with you. If you're like most people and want the ads you see to be more relevant, take a look at your ad preferences by clicking “Why am I seeing this?” in the upper right corner of any ad on Facebook. For example, if “dance” is one of your interests and you want to see fewer ads about “dance,” deselect it in ad preferences or choose something else. Your ad preferences will only be visible to you.


* * *


If you live in the U.S., you'll be able to use ad preferences in the next few weeks, and we are working hard to expand globally in the coming months.

Data Privacy Day
By Erin Egan, Chief Privacy Officer

Every year on January 28, Facebook, along with hundreds of companies, organizations, governments and educational institutions, marks Data Privacy Day, a global effort to promote awareness around data and privacy.


With more than one billion people using Facebook around the world, we think it's important that we help... people understand and take control of their information and the things they share and do.  This kind of empowerment is fundamental to our mission of making the world more open and connected. By helping people navigate life in a connected world, we hope not only to help illuminate the power of this connectivity, but the important ways you can protect and empower yourself as you explore the opportunities of our data-driven age.   As Facebook’s Chief Privacy Officer, Policy, I'm pleased that Data Privacy Day gives me another opportunity to talk about these important issues and help shine a spotlight on them.   


All this information sharing – whether on Facebook or with other services – has tangible benefits for businesses, for people, and for society. For example, you can use Facebook to launch and grow a business, to find support in a time of need, to donate to a favorite charity or help with hurricane relief efforts. Facebook helps you share information with the people you choose.  By doing so, you’re creating richer, more meaningful connections with people and ideas you care about. 


It's important to Facebook that people using our service understand the many ways you can control who sees the information you choose to share. Take our audience selection tool, for example. This tool gives you the ability to select your audience for just about everything you post on Facebook – often right when you post.  This way, you know what you are sharing with your friends, family and other connections. The tool is simple and powerful, because it allows you to tell your own story in your own way, right when you post. 


Watch the video about choosing your audience on Facebook here: https://www.facebook.com/photo.php?v=782308178464316


This year, I am proud to help kick off Data Privacy Day from Washington, D.C., where I will join other privacy experts to discuss "A Competitive Advantage: Respecting Consumer Privacy in a Big Data World.” I also invite you to join today’s conversation, as we stream it live on the Data Privacy: NCSA Facebook page (https://www.facebook.com/DataPrivacy) starting at 11:20am ET.

An Update to Facebook Ads

Last year, we announced some changes to simplify Facebook ads, including eliminating different types of ads that had the same purpose and making our ads look more consistent. We also announced that marketers will no longer be able to purchase sponsored stories separately; instead, social context — stories about social actions your friends have take...n, such as liking a page or checking in to a restaurant — is now eligible to appear next to all ads shown to friends on Facebook. 


This week, we gave notice to our ad partners that this change relating to sponsored stories will start in the first quarter of this year so they can update their tools and continue supporting the marketers they work with. 


So what does this mean for people on Facebook? As before, you are in control of who sees what you post on Facebook, whether it appears in News Feed, next to ads, or elsewhere on Facebook. You can visit your Activity Log to see who can see stories about your social actions and change the audience or unlike or delete the content at any time. 


In addition, you can visit your Ads and Friends setting to limit when stories about your social actions are paired with ads shown to friends.


To learn more about your Activity log, visit https://www.facebook.com/help/www/437430672945092?rdrhc.


To learn more about your Ads and Friends settings, visit https://www.facebook.com/settings?tab=ads&section=social&view.


You can also visit our Advertising on Facebook page here: https://www.facebook.com/about/ads/.


And, our Help Center here: https://www.facebook.com/help/.
